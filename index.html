Susan Collins (R)

Incumbent US Senator

Background

Susan Collins is the Republican incumbent U.S. Senator from Maine, first elected in 1996 and now serving her fifth term; she chairs the Senate Appropriations Committee, having previously worked on the staff of former Sen. William S. Cohen and served as deputy state treasurer of Massachusetts before her Senate career. (source)

Current storyline

The defining story in the race has shifted from Collins herself to the turmoil in the Democratic field: nominee Graham Platner, who won the June 9 primary after Gov. Janet Mills suspended her campaign, is facing a new sexual assault allegation that he denies, prompting numerous Democratic leaders and groups, including VoteVets and the Working Families Party, to rescind endorsements and call on him to withdraw. Collins has largely stayed above the fray while touting her seniority and Appropriations Committee chairmanship, and has received supportive comments from President Trump, who has said he would campaign for her. Coverage from the Washington Post, Axios, CNBC, and the Associated Press has focused heavily on whether Platner will remain in the race and who might replace him if he exits, given Maine's status as one of the most competitive Senate contests of the cycle.

Graham Platner (D)

Oyster farmer, Marine veteran

Background

Graham Platner is an oyster farmer and Marine Corps and Army National Guard veteran who served combat tours in Iraq and Afghanistan before returning to Maine to run an oyster farming operation and serve as a local harbormaster; he won the Democratic nomination for U.S. Senate in Maine on June 9, 2026, defeating David Costello after Gov. Janet Mills exited the race. (source)

Current storyline

Platner's campaign has been upended by a Politico report in which Jenny Racicot, a woman he previously dated, alleged he forced himself on her sexually in 2021; Platner has denied the allegation as "categorically false" while saying he is taking time to "reflect on the best path forward" for his candidacy. In response, a wave of prominent Democratic endorsers—including Senate Democratic leader Chuck Schumer, DSCC chair Kirsten Gillibrand, Sens. Ruben Gallego and Elizabeth Warren, Rep. Ro Khanna, and the Maine Democratic Party—rescinded their support and called for him to withdraw, and Sen. Bernie Sanders, one of his earliest and most prominent backers, said he had recommended Platner step aside. Under Maine law Platner would need to withdraw by July 13 for the state Democratic Party to name a replacement nominee by July 27, and press coverage has focused on potential replacement candidates such as Troy Jackson and Shenna Bellows.
